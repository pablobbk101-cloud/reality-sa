Load Shedding

South Africa has endured rolling blackouts since 2007. What began as a warning sign has become a permanent feature of daily life — costing the economy billions and pushing millions into hardship every year.

A Grid on the Brink

Load shedding — the deliberate, rotational cutting of electricity supply — has become one of South Africa's most defining and damaging crises. Managed by the state-owned utility Eskom, the national grid is burdened by decades of deferred maintenance, mismanagement, and corruption that have left the country unable to meet its own demand for power.

At its worst, Stage 6 load shedding means households and businesses lose power for up to 12 hours every day. Factories halt production. Food spoils. Medical equipment fails. Petrol station queues stretch around the block as people wait for the power to run pumps. For the millions who cannot afford generators or inverters, there is simply darkness.

"Load shedding is not just an inconvenience — it is a tax on the poor, a handbrake on the economy, and a symbol of institutional failure at the highest level."

Why Is It Happening?

The roots of the crisis lie in decisions made — and not made — over two decades. During the late 1990s and early 2000s, warnings from engineers that South Africa needed new power stations were ignored. By 2007, the inevitable happened: the grid could no longer keep up with demand.

Two new mega-stations, Medupi and Kusile, were commissioned to solve the problem. Both projects became symbols of everything wrong with Eskom — plagued by cost overruns, construction defects, and allegations of corruption. Medupi cost over R145 billion, more than triple the original budget. Kusile remains incomplete.

Meanwhile, the existing coal fleet — which provides roughly 80% of South Africa's electricity — has aged and degraded. Breakdowns have become the norm rather than the exception. Eskom refers to this as "unplanned capacity loss" — a polite term for a fleet that is falling apart.

Timeline of the Crisis

2007
First major blackouts South Africa experiences its first widespread load shedding. Government and Eskom acknowledge the grid is under severe strain.
2008
National emergency declared Mines shut down. The economy loses an estimated R50 billion. Medupi and Kusile power stations are approved.
2014
Shedding returns, worse than before Rolling blackouts resume after a period of relief. Stage 3 becomes a new reality for South African households.
2019
Stage 6 introduced South Africa reaches Stage 6 for the first time — the highest level at that point, meaning up to 6,000 MW removed from the grid simultaneously.
2022
Record-breaking outages South Africa spends more days in load shedding than not. Stage 6 becomes almost routine. Economic losses exceed R1 billion per day.
2023–24
Gradual improvement Private solar investment surges. Eskom reports some maintenance progress. Load shedding begins to ease but the structural problems remain unresolved.

Who Bears the Cost?

The burden of load shedding falls heaviest on those who can least afford it. Wealthy households invest in solar panels, battery backups, and generators. Poor households — often in townships and rural areas — endure the darkness with nothing to cushion the blow.

Small businesses have been devastated. Restaurants discard spoiled stock. Informal traders cannot operate equipment. Barbershops and salons close mid-appointment. The cumulative economic damage to the small business sector runs into the hundreds of billions of rands.

For students, load shedding disrupts study time, makes internet access unreliable, and widens the gap between those with resources and those without. South Africa's education crisis is being deepened by its energy crisis.

Who Is Affected
  • Households — food spoilage, no heating or cooling
  • Small businesses — lost trading hours, spoiled stock
  • Schools — disrupted lessons and exams
  • Hospitals — reliance on costly backup generators
  • Water pumping stations — secondary water shortages
  • Farms — irrigation failures, livestock losses
Key Numbers
  • R1bn+ estimated daily economic loss at Stage 6
  • Over 200 days of load shedding in 2022 alone
  • R145bn+ spent on Medupi — over 3× original budget
  • ~80% of SA electricity still generated by coal
  • Solar installations grew by 400%+ in 2022–23
