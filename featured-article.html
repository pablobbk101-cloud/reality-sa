South Africa vs Israel:
Why We Shouldn't
Be Involved.

Mr EchoPoint — March 8, 2026 • 8 min read

South Africa barged into the ICJ case against Israel, screaming "genocide." Headlines blew up, the world noticed, and suddenly we're centre stage. But let's be real: this isn't our war. It's not our fight. And dragging ourselves into it is reckless, clout-chasing nonsense.

🚫 Not Our War

I understand why people get emotional about Gaza. The images are horrific. The civilian death toll is devastating. Nobody with a conscience looks at this conflict and feels nothing. But emotion is not foreign policy — and it definitely isn't a substitute for looking after your own country first.

South Africa didn't just express solidarity or issue a statement. We took Israel to the highest court in the world. We filed 750 pages of legal argument. We've been building a case for over two years. This is a massive, ongoing commitment of diplomatic capital, legal resources, and international political energy.

And my question is simple: what exactly are we getting out of this?

🏚️ Fix Home Before Playing Hero

Here's what's happening in South Africa right now while Ramaphosa is busy at The Hague:

Youth unemployment is sitting above 60%. Crime is out of control. Load shedding is still wrecking lives and businesses. Corruption is baked so deep into the system that we've forgotten what clean governance looks like.

These aren't small problems. These are existential crises for millions of South Africans who are waiting — desperately waiting — for their government to show up for them. Instead, Ramaphosa wants to play global saviour.

South Africa doesn't need to cosplay as the world's moral compass while our own house is burning. Every rand spent on this legal campaign. Every hour of government attention. Every diplomatic relationship strained by this stance. That's a cost being paid by ordinary South Africans who needed that energy directed at home.

🎭 Ramaphosa's Ego Trip

Let's call it what it is. Ramaphosa loves the spotlight. He wants to look like Mandela 2.0 on the world stage — the great statesman, the moral voice of the Global South. But at home? He's failing hard.

He's weak domestically, so he flexes internationally. He borrows moral credibility from our apartheid history instead of earning it through actual governance today. He's turning South Africa's hard-won reputation into his personal PR campaign.

Think about the optics. While South African citizens deal with rolling blackouts, crumbling public hospitals, and a justice system that can't convict the powerful — their president is flying to The Hague to lecture the world about international law. It's not leadership. It's performance.

And here's the deeper problem: it's selective performance. When Russia invaded Ukraine, South Africa abstained. When human rights abuses happen in Zimbabwe — our neighbour — we look away. When Zuma hosted Putin despite an ICC arrest warrant, we blinked. But Israel? Suddenly we find our moral backbone.

That inconsistency doesn't go unnoticed. It tells the world our "principles" are actually politics dressed up in legal language.

💸 The Real Cost

This isn't just symbolic. Our involvement comes with real fallout that affects real South Africans:

Diplomatic damage. The United States — one of our most significant trading partners — has called the case meritless and counterproductive. US lawmakers have floated sanctions against South Africa. Whatever you think about American foreign policy, picking a prolonged legal fight with Washington is not without consequences for a country that desperately needs investment.

Economic risk. We are not a wealthy country. We cannot afford trade disruptions, investment hesitancy, or being seen as geopolitically unstable. Our economy is fragile. The rand is fragile. The idea that we can absorb the diplomatic fallout of this case without economic consequences is naive.

Credibility damage. When people look at the inconsistency — silent on Russia, vocal on Israel — they don't see principle. They see politics. And once that perception takes hold internationally, it's hard to shake. We're gambling our reputation for a war that doesn't even touch our borders.

🔥 Priorities, People

Imagine if all this energy went into fixing South Africa instead. Imagine if the legal brilliance that went into a 750-page ICJ memorial went into prosecuting the corrupt politicians who have stolen hundreds of billions from this country. Imagine if the diplomatic energy went into attracting investment, creating jobs, building infrastructure.

Jobs for the millions stuck hustling with nothing. Power that actually stays on. Real accountability for corrupt politicians. Safer streets for ordinary people.

That's where history will judge us. Not in The Hague. In Soweto. In Khayelitsha. In every township where people are still waiting for change that was promised thirty years ago.

📢 My Stand

I'm against South Africa's involvement in this case. Period.

Not because justice doesn't matter. Not because Gaza isn't a tragedy. Not because international law is unimportant. But because priorities matter. Because a government has a primary obligation to its own citizens. Because we cannot afford — financially, diplomatically, or morally — to be the world's conscience when we haven't yet become our own people's government.

Ramaphosa needs to back off. South Africa needs to step aside. Let the world fight its battles. We've got more than enough of our own.

🕰️ Final Word

History will remember this moment. But it won't just ask what we did — it'll ask why. And if the answer is "because Ramaphosa wanted the spotlight," then we'll look like fools who traded our credibility for his ego trip.

South Africa's voice in the world should be earned through what we build at home — not borrowed from the legacy of people who actually suffered under oppression. Until we fix what's broken here, we have no business lecturing anyone.
