The House at the End of the Unmade Road

A man returns to a house that shouldn't exist anymore. What he finds inside are rooms that remember things he never told anyone.

The road wasn't on any map. Kofi had checked three times — on his phone, on the printed directions his aunt had written for him in her careful handwriting, and once more on the older map he'd found folded inside the glove compartment of his father's car. The road wasn't there. But the road was there.

It began between two msasa trees so large their roots had lifted the tar on either side. Beyond them, the track was red clay and stones, unmarked by any signpost, narrowing as it curved out of sight. Kofi sat with the engine running for a long moment, watching the way the late-afternoon light moved through the branches overhead.

He had not been back here in eleven years. Not since the fire. Not since the night he'd stood at the end of this same road and watched orange light fill every window and understood, without being told, that certain things do not survive.

· · ·

The house was standing.

That was the first impossible thing. The second was the light on in the kitchen window, warm and yellow and completely ordinary, as if someone were inside making tea.

Kofi switched off the engine. In the silence that followed he could hear the ticking of the cooling metal, the sound of birds settling into the trees, and underneath all of it, something else — a low, almost inaudible hum, like a house makes when it is full of people breathing.

He got out of the car. The air smelled of rain that hadn't fallen yet and something else, something harder to name. Woodsmoke, maybe. Or the particular smell of old books left in a room that doesn't get enough sun.

· · ·

The front door opened before he reached it. No one was there — only the door, swinging back on quiet hinges, and the hallway beyond it: the same blue-painted walls his mother had chosen, the same dark wood floor, the same photograph of his grandparents hanging where it had always hung.

In the photograph, his grandfather was looking at something outside the frame. His grandmother had her hand on his arm. Both of them were wearing the kind of careful, dressed-up clothes that meant an occasion worth remembering. Kofi had looked at this photograph a thousand times growing up and never once asked what the occasion was.

He asked now, to the empty hallway. "What was it? What were you celebrating?"

And the house, very quietly, began to answer.
